# Science & Discovery Feature

**Platform:** Science / Nature / Discovery Features
**Type:** Science, nature, archaeology, and discovery content
**Primary personas:** [§4.3 The Wonder-Driven Science Enthusiast]({{ "/docs/science-enthusiast" | relative_url }}) · [§4.4 The Curious Explorer]({{ "/docs/curious-explorer" | relative_url }})

> **Scope:** This page documents editorial writing standards for Science & Discovery Feature content — pieces that surface scientific discoveries, nature revelations, and archaeological findings for wonder-driven readers. These guidelines direct the CSA's general-style agent. CSA product-level behaviors (locked headline formula, URL pattern, tags) are not yet specified for this format — follow General Guidelines §1 as the default for all unspecified elements.
>
> **Two audience modes:** This format serves two distinct but related personas. The **Science Enthusiast** mode (§4.3) targets broad awe-driven readers across all discovery topics. The **Explorer** mode (§4.4) targets deep-engagement readers drawn to specific, shareable discovery moments — new species, archaeology, "weird science." Use the audience selector in the CSA to choose between them.

### Purpose

A Science & Discovery Feature reveals something extraordinary that the reader didn't know existed — and makes them feel the significance of the discovery. Its primary purpose is to convey awe, expand understanding, and give readers a fact, finding, or insight they genuinely want to share. The piece should leave readers feeling smarter and more connected to the world's hidden complexity.

<!-- AGENT-AUDIENCE: general-style -->
### Content Framework
**(REQUIRED)**

Each Science & Discovery Feature must address all four points in sequence. The CSA uses this framework to shape how it generates and evaluates content for both the Science Enthusiast and Curious Explorer personas.

- **Discovery — What It Is:** Define the discovery, finding, or phenomenon in one vivid, jargon-free line — make readers feel the significance immediately
- **Understanding — Why It Matters:** Explain why it expands understanding, challenges assumptions, or stirs genuine emotion — not just what was found, but what it means
- **Evaluation — Who It's For:** Universalize the wonder — frame it as something any curious mind would find significant, regardless of prior knowledge or expertise
- **Action — How to Experience It:** Offer ways to explore further: museum exhibits, documentaries, imagery, related readings, or context that invites continued wonder

<!-- AGENT-AUDIENCE: general-style -->
### Tone
**(REQUIRED)**

Accessible, vivid, and story-forward. Lead with marvel, not data — explanation follows awe, not the reverse. Respect the reader's intelligence; explain without condescending. Pacing and clarity must sustain curiosity from first line to last.

**Science Enthusiast mode:** Broader, more meditative — sustain the sense of wonder throughout; connect findings to the reader's emotional and intellectual experience of the world.

**Explorer mode:** More immediate and kinetic — hook with the specific detail, move through the discovery fast, give the reader their shareable fact clearly and early.

<!-- AGENT-AUDIENCE: general-style -->
### Writing Guidance

**Universal (both modes):**
- Lead with awe, not data — marvel first, explanation second
- Prioritize comprehension and emotional resonance over technical depth
- Showcase the extraordinary within the knowable — revelations about familiar things outperform pure exotica
- Write with pacing and clarity that sustains curiosity from first line to last
- Respect the reader's intelligence — explain, don't condescend

**Science Enthusiast mode (§4.3):**
- Topics across paleontology, marine life, astronomy, and environmental change — each has distinct registers; tailor accordingly
- Vivid, jargon-free discovery stories that make readers feel the significance of what's been found
- "Extraordinary within the familiar" — revelations about things readers thought they already knew
- Tie-ins that invite continued wonder: museum exhibits, documentaries, imagery, related readings

**Explorer mode (§4.4):**
- Lead every piece with the single most jaw-dropping specific detail — a number, a scale, a physical description
- Frame articles as answering a question the reader didn't know they had
- Animals with personality and narrative arc dramatically outperform dry taxonomy
- Prioritize topics where science reveals something hidden or overturns a prior assumption
- Headlines should combine specificity with a knowledge gap: "A 4-Foot Snake Killed by a Hiker Turned Out to Be Something Scientists Had Never Seen Before"
- Avoid generic nature roundups — every piece needs a concrete, surprising hook

### What to Avoid

| Prohibited | Reason |
|---|---|
| Leading with data before awe | Comprehension and emotional resonance are the primary goals — data that precedes wonder loses the reader |
| Jargon without plain-language translation | Accessibility is required — technical terms must always be followed by a human explanation |
| Generic nature roundups without a concrete hook | Curiosity Explorer persona specifically requires a specific, surprising hook — roundups don't deliver it |
| Condescending explanation | Reader intelligence is assumed — explain for clarity, not remediation |
| Pure exotica with no accessible anchor | Revelations about familiar things outperform pure novelty — always provide an emotional or familiar reference point |

### Pre-Publish Checklist

- Discovery defined in one vivid, jargon-free line
- Why it matters stated explicitly — not just what was found, but what it means
- Awe established before data — marvel leads, explanation follows
- Wonder universalized — framed for any curious reader regardless of expertise
- Further exploration offered (exhibit, documentary, imagery, or related reading)
- For Explorer mode: single jaw-dropping detail leads the piece; shareable fact is clear
- For Science Enthusiast mode: emotional resonance sustained throughout; tone is accessible and story-forward
- No jargon without plain-language follow-up
- Named human byline — creator/first editor only
- All facts verified; all links point to reputable sources
- Hero image appropriate, striking, and non-generic
- AI disclaimer present (CUE sites) and "Created With AI" checkbox checked in CUE if applicable
- Human review and approval obtained before publishing
